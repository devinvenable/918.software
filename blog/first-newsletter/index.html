What Happens When AI Replaces You

January 15, 2025

When I was recently laid off from a full-time role, it hit me: I'd been replaced by AI.

Let me explain. I was the founding engineer at a startup—my fifth time in that role during my career. Startups are risky, but rewarding. You get to build from scratch, but you're racing the clock. Most don't make it.

In past startups, I was always the last one out. You need someone around who can put out fires, upgrade systems, and access the data. This time, I wasn't the last one out.

I advocated for hiring a prompt engineer skilled in 'Vibe Coding,' a practice where users leverage AI prompts to build code, sometimes without fully grasping its mechanics. This individual's proactive approach and AI proficiency impressed me significantly. I communicated to leadership that their output, facilitated by AI, surpassed that of certain traditional engineers I've collaborated with.

And then cuts came and I was the one to go.

No hard feelings. I get it—business is business. If they could get similar output from someone who costs less and shift budget toward sales, it made sense.

Still, losing a salary stings. And for the first time in my career, finding the next thing wasn't instant. Development jobs in 2025 are harder to get.

It's not just me. Big Tech saw massive layoffs in 2024—around 95,000 jobs cut—and it's still happening in 2025. Companies like Intel, Tesla, and Cisco scaled back after years of aggressive hiring.

Development roles are changing dramatically in 2025

A lot of those cuts hit software engineers. The reasons vary—economic shifts, less venture capital—but AI is clearly part of the story. Salesforce has hinted at slowing down dev hiring thanks to AI productivity gains. Even Google's CEO said AI is now generating a big chunk of their code.

It feels less like a blip—and more like a shift.

So, what next? What did I learn—and how would I pivot?

While I've long been an advocate for AI, I remain passionate about writing code. Though I've consistently been a highly productive developer—a demonstrable fact, not a boast—I recognize that even my expertise cannot match the speed of a machine. Therefore, I've decided to prioritize English as my primary programming interface.

The result? In the past five weeks, I've built a lot. With my experience and a lightning-fast AI sidekick, progress is real.

No-Job.Dev

One project is no-job.dev—a space for developers out of work. I saw so many devs on LinkedIn saying they were months into a job hunt with no luck. That's a lot of noise for a platform not built for that kind of vulnerability, so I launched a site to give it a home.

What to expect when you weren't expecting

In 2023, there were over 1.6 million software devs in the U.S. I think we're on the edge of a major shift—especially for those not embracing AI. No-job.dev is early, but I'm building it to be ready for that shift.

Meanwhile, over at 918.software, the lab is buzzing. We’re prototyping a booking app for pool cleaners (because even robots need a clean swim), tinkering with a blockchain-based private messenger (for when you want privacy and receipts), and smoothing out the chaos of social media with tools that make Instagram patterns easier to repurpose elsewhere.

There’s also a lightweight video clip editor in the works—built for fast, no-fuss storytelling—and we’re deep in the weeds distilling large language models into something leaner and meaner for custom use cases.

This is what happens when you give a builder AI superpowers and a little free time.

We're also shifting from just custom dev work to helping teams train AI, integrate it, and automate workflows.

If you're into this stuff, I'd love to keep you on the newsletter. But no pressure—opt out anytime.

Peace,
Devin
